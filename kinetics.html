Inorganic Chemistry: Kinetics

Chemical kinetics is the study of reaction rates and mechanisms. It's one of the professor's frequently tested topics and requires understanding both theoretical concepts and practical applications.

Reaction Rates

Definition and Measurement

  • Reaction rate: Change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time
  • Average rate: Δ[A]/Δt (negative for reactants, positive for products)
  • Instantaneous rate: d[A]/dt (the limit as Δt approaches zero)
  • Units: Usually mol/(L·s) or M/s

Rate Law

  • General form: Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n...
  • Rate constant (k): Proportionality constant specific to each reaction
  • Reaction order:
    • m = order with respect to A
    • n = order with respect to B
    • m + n + ... = overall reaction order
  • Determination: By experimental data (method of initial rates)

Reaction Order

Zero-Order Reactions

  • Rate law: Rate = k
  • Integrated rate law: [A]t = [A]0 - kt
  • Half-life: t1/2 = [A]0/(2k)
  • Characteristics: Rate is independent of concentration
  • Examples: Surface-catalyzed reactions, photochemical reactions

First-Order Reactions

  • Rate law: Rate = k[A]
  • Integrated rate law: ln[A]t = ln[A]0 - kt
  • Half-life: t1/2 = ln(2)/k (independent of initial concentration)
  • Characteristics: Rate is directly proportional to concentration
  • Examples: Radioactive decay, many decomposition reactions

Second-Order Reactions

  • Rate law: Rate = k[A]^2 or Rate = k[A][B]
  • Integrated rate law (for [A]^2): 1/[A]t = 1/[A]0 + kt
  • Half-life: t1/2 = 1/(k[A]0) (depends on initial concentration)
  • Characteristics: Rate is proportional to the square of concentration or product of two concentrations
  • Examples: Many bimolecular reactions

Mnemonic: "ZERO-ONE-TWO"

  • Zero Order: straight line plot of [A] vs. time, t₁/₂ ∝ [A]₀
  • One Order: straight line plot of ln[A] vs. time, t₁/₂ = constant
  • Two Order: straight line plot of 1/[A] vs. time, t₁/₂ ∝ 1/[A]₀

Factors Affecting Reaction Rates

Concentration

  • Higher concentration: More frequent molecular collisions, faster reaction
  • Quantitative relationship: Determined by the reaction order

Temperature

  • Effect: Higher temperature generally increases reaction rate
  • Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)
    • k = rate constant
    • A = frequency factor (pre-exponential factor)
    • Ea = activation energy
    • R = gas constant
    • T = absolute temperature
  • Logarithmic form: ln(k) = ln(A) - Ea/RT
  • Graphical determination: Plot ln(k) vs. 1/T gives a straight line with slope = -Ea/R

Memory Aid: "KATE"

  • K = A × Temperature factor × Energy factor
  • K (rate constant) increases when:
  • A (frequency factor) increases
  • Temperature increases
  • Ea (activation energy) decreases

Catalysts

  • Definition: Substances that increase reaction rate without being consumed
  • Function: Lower the activation energy by providing an alternative reaction pathway
  • Types:
    • Homogeneous catalysts: Same phase as reactants
    • Heterogeneous catalysts: Different phase from reactants
    • Enzymes: Biological catalysts with high specificity

Reaction Mechanisms

Elementary Steps

  • Definition: Reaction steps that occur exactly as written in the molecular equation
  • Types:
    • Unimolecular: Involve one molecule (A → products)
    • Bimolecular: Involve two molecules (A + B → products)
    • Termolecular: Involve three molecules (rare due to improbability of simultaneous collision)

Molecularity vs. Order

  • Molecularity: Number of molecules that must collide in an elementary step
  • Order: Experimentally determined exponents in the rate law
  • Relationship: For elementary steps, molecularity equals order

Rate-Determining Step

  • Definition: Slowest step in a multi-step mechanism
  • Significance: Controls the overall reaction rate
  • Steady-state approximation: Assumes intermediates are consumed as quickly as they are formed

Tip: Connecting Rate Laws to Mechanisms

  • The rate-determining step determines the overall rate law
  • If A must collide with B in the slow step, the rate depends on [A][B]
  • If A must first dissociate (slow step) before reacting with B, the rate depends only on [A]
  • Think of it like a highway with a bottleneck—the narrowest point determines overall traffic flow

Collision Theory

Basic Principles

  • Requirements for reaction:
    • Molecules must collide
    • Collision must have sufficient energy (≥ activation energy)
    • Molecules must have proper orientation

Effective Collisions

  • Definition: Collisions that lead to product formation
  • Factors affecting:
    • Collision frequency (depends on concentration and temperature)
    • Fraction of collisions with sufficient energy (e^(-Ea/RT))
    • Steric factor (proper orientation)

Activation Energy

  • Definition: Minimum energy required for a reaction to occur
  • Energy profile: Graphical representation of energy changes during reaction
  • Transition state: Highest energy configuration along reaction pathway
  • Activated complex: Unstable intermediate at the transition state

Tip: Understanding Activation Energy

Think of activation energy as a hill that molecules must climb:

  • Higher hill = fewer molecules have enough energy to react
  • Catalysts create a tunnel through the hill rather than lowering it
  • Temperature increases the "running speed" of all molecules
