Biochemistry Propedeutics: NAD and FAD

NAD (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide) and FAD (Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide) are essential coenzymes involved in redox reactions in metabolism. Your professor frequently asks about these structures, so understanding them in detail is critical for your exam success.

Important: This is one of the most frequently tested topics in your oral exam! Make sure you can draw the complete structures of NAD+ and FAD, explain their redox properties, and describe their roles in metabolism.

NAD Structure and Properties

Chemical Structure

  • Components:
    • Nicotinamide (derived from vitamin B3/niacin)
    • Adenine (purine base)
    • Two ribose sugars
    • Two phosphate groups
  • Linkage: Pyrophosphate bridge connects the two nucleotides
  • Nomenclature: NAD⁺ (oxidized form), NADH (reduced form)

Detailed Structure

  • Nicotinamide nucleotide:
    • Nicotinamide connected to ribose at N-1
    • Ribose connected to phosphate at C-5'
  • Adenine nucleotide:
    • Adenine connected to ribose at N-9
    • Ribose connected to phosphate at C-5'
  • Connection: The two phosphate groups form a pyrophosphate bridge

Mnemonic: "NAPPY"

  • Nicotinamide (from vitamin B3)
  • Adenine (purine base)
  • Phosphate groups (two)
  • Pentose sugars (two ribose units)
  • Yoked together by pyrophosphate bridge

NAD Redox Properties

Redox Center

The nicotinamide ring is the redox-active part of NAD.

Oxidized form (NAD⁺)

  • Positive charge on nitrogen
  • Accepts two electrons and one proton

Reduced form (NADH)

  • Neutral nicotinamide
  • Contains two additional electrons and one proton
NAD⁺ + 2e⁻ + H⁺ → NADH

Standard reduction potential: -0.32 V

Spectroscopic Properties

  • Absorption:
    • NAD⁺: Absorbs at 260 nm
    • NADH: Absorbs at 260 nm and 340 nm
  • Fluorescence:
    • NAD⁺: Non-fluorescent
    • NADH: Fluorescent (emission at 460 nm)

The spectroscopic differences between NAD⁺ and NADH allow scientists to monitor redox reactions in real-time. For example, in enzyme assays, the appearance of absorbance at 340 nm indicates the formation of NADH, which can be used to calculate reaction rates.

NADP Structure and Properties

Structural Difference from NAD

  • Additional phosphate: Attached to 2'-hydroxyl of adenosine ribose
  • Nomenclature: NADP⁺ (oxidized form), NADPH (reduced form)

Functional Difference

  • NAD⁺/NADH: Primarily involved in catabolic (energy-yielding) reactions
  • NADP⁺/NADPH: Primarily involved in anabolic (biosynthetic) reactions

NADPH is used in fatty acid and steroid synthesis. The additional phosphate group allows enzymes to distinguish between NAD and NADP, directing them to different metabolic pathways.

FAD Structure and Properties

Chemical Structure

  • Components:
    • Flavin (isoalloxazine ring system derived from riboflavin/vitamin B2)
    • Ribitol (sugar alcohol)
    • Adenine (purine base)
    • Ribose sugar
    • Two phosphate groups
  • Linkage: Phosphate groups connect ribitol to ribose
  • Nomenclature: FAD (oxidized form), FADH₂ (reduced form)

Mnemonic: "FAIR"

  • Flavin (isoalloxazine ring)
  • Adenine (purine base)
  • Interconnected by phosphates
  • Ribitol links flavin to phosphate

FAD Redox Properties

Redox Center

The isoalloxazine ring system is the redox-active part of FAD.

Oxidized form (FAD)

  • Fully conjugated ring system
  • Yellow color

Reduced form (FADH₂)

  • Two additional electrons and two protons
  • Colorless
FAD + 2e⁻ + 2H⁺ → FADH₂

Standard reduction potential: -0.22 V

Spectroscopic Properties

  • Absorption:
    • FAD: Absorbs at 450 nm (yellow)
    • FADH₂: Minimal absorption in visible range (colorless)
  • Fluorescence:
    • FAD: Weakly fluorescent
    • FADH₂: Non-fluorescent

The color change from yellow (FAD) to colorless (FADH₂) allows visual monitoring of redox reactions. This is particularly useful in enzyme assays where FAD-dependent enzymes are being studied.

Comparison of NAD and FAD

Structural Differences

  • Redox-active group:
    • NAD: Nicotinamide (pyridine derivative)
    • FAD: Isoalloxazine (flavin)
  • Electron transfer:
    • NAD: Transfers two electrons simultaneously (hydride ion)
    • FAD: Can transfer electrons one at a time (radical intermediate possible)
