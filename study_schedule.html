20-Day Study Schedule

This schedule is designed to help you systematically cover all the required material for your chemistry oral exam over a 20-day period. It allocates extra time to the frequently asked topics and ensures balanced coverage of inorganic chemistry, organic chemistry, and biochemistry propedeutics.

Week 1: Inorganic Chemistry Foundations

Day 1-2: Introduction and Atomic Structure

  • Day 1: Scientific method, matter classification, atomic theory
  • Day 2: Atomic structure, periodic table, electron configuration

Focus: Understand the fundamental concepts that form the basis for more complex topics. Pay special attention to electron configurations and periodic trends.

Create a periodic table map highlighting electron configurations and trends. Practice drawing orbital diagrams for different elements. Review the historical development of atomic theory to understand how concepts evolved.

Day 3-4: Chemical Bonding

  • Day 3: Ionic and covalent bonding, Lewis structures
  • Day 4: Molecular geometry, hybridization, molecular orbital theory

Focus: Master drawing Lewis structures and predicting molecular geometry. Understand the relationship between hybridization and molecular shape.

Practice drawing Lewis structures for at least 20 different molecules. Create 3D models of molecules with different geometries. Create a chart comparing different types of bonds and their properties.

Week 2: Thermodynamics, Kinetics, and Solutions

Day 5-7: Thermodynamics

  • Day 5: First law, enthalpy, Hess's law
  • Day 6: Entropy, spontaneity, Gibbs free energy
  • Day 7: Thermodynamic equilibrium, practice problems

Focus: This is a frequently tested topic! Understand the relationships between ΔH, ΔS, and ΔG. Practice calculating thermodynamic quantities and predicting reaction spontaneity.

Create a thermodynamic cycle diagram for visualizing Hess's law. Practice predicting spontaneity using the "PETS" rule. Work through multiple calculation problems involving ΔG = ΔH - TΔS.

Day 8-9: Kinetics

  • Day 8: Reaction rates, rate laws, reaction order
  • Day 9: Arrhenius equation, reaction mechanisms, catalysis

Focus: Another frequently tested topic! Master determining reaction orders and writing rate laws. Understand how to use the Arrhenius equation to relate temperature and rate constants.

Create a chart comparing zero, first, and second-order reactions. Practice drawing energy diagrams for reactions with and without catalysts. Work through problems involving the determination of activation energy.

Day 10: Solutions and Equilibrium

  • Solution composition and properties
  • Colligative properties
  • Chemical equilibrium and Le Chatelier's principle

Focus: Understand how to calculate solution concentrations and apply Le Chatelier's principle to predict equilibrium shifts.

Practice concentration calculations using different units (molarity, molality, etc.). Create a flowchart for predicting equilibrium shifts using Le Chatelier's principle. Review the relationship between Kc and Kp.

Week 3: Electrochemistry and Organic Chemistry

Day 11-12: Electrochemistry

  • Day 11: Electrolytic conduction, electrolysis, galvanic cells
  • Day 12: Electrode potentials, Nernst equation, applications

Focus: This is one of the most frequently tested topics! Master drawing cell diagrams, calculating cell potentials, and applying the Nernst equation.

Practice drawing galvanic and electrolytic cells. Create a table of standard reduction potentials for common half-reactions. Work through problems involving the Nernst equation at non-standard conditions.

Day 13-15: Organic Chemistry

  • Day 13: Alkanes, isomerism, stereochemistry
  • Day 14: Functional groups: alkenes, alkynes, alcohols
  • Day 15: Carbonyl compounds, carboxylic acids, aromatic compounds

Focus: Master drawing structures and reaction mechanisms. Pay special attention to stereochemistry and the reactivity of different functional groups.

Practice drawing and naming compounds with different functional groups. Create reaction maps showing transformations between functional groups. Draw mechanisms for key reactions like nucleophilic substitution and electrophilic addition.

Week 4: Biochemistry Propedeutics and Final Review

Day 16-17: Carbohydrates

  • Day 16: Monosaccharides: structure, properties, derivatives
  • Day 17: Disaccharides, polysaccharides, biological importance

Focus: This is a frequently tested topic! Master drawing the structures of common monosaccharides in different forms (linear, cyclic). Understand the formation of glycosidic bonds.

Practice drawing glucose in both linear and cyclic forms. Create a chart comparing different monosaccharides. Draw the structures of common disaccharides showing the glycosidic linkages.

Day 18: NAD and FAD

  • Structure and properties of NAD and FAD
  • Redox characteristics and biochemical significance
  • Comparison of NAD and FAD

Focus: This is one of the most frequently tested topics! Master drawing the structures of NAD and FAD. Understand their roles in redox reactions and metabolic pathways.

Practice drawing the complete structures of NAD+ and FAD. Create a comparison chart highlighting the structural and functional differences. Draw the redox reactions involving these coenzymes.

Day 19-20: Comprehensive Review and Practice

  • Day 19: Review of frequently tested topics, practice explaining concepts aloud
  • Day 20: Final review, practice drawing key structures and reactions

Focus: Consolidate your understanding of all topics, with special emphasis on the frequently tested areas. Practice explaining concepts as you would in an oral exam.

Create a one-page summary for each major topic. Practice explaining concepts to a friend or recording yourself. Review all mnemonics and memory aids. Ensure you can draw all important structures and reactions without reference materials.
