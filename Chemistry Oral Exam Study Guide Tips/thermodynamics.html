Inorganic Chemistry: Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the branch of chemistry that deals with the relationships between heat, work, and energy. It's one of the most frequently tested topics in your oral exam and requires both conceptual understanding and mathematical proficiency.

Thermodynamic Systems and Variables

Thermodynamic Systems

  • Open system: Can exchange both matter and energy with surroundings
  • Closed system: Can exchange energy but not matter with surroundings
  • Isolated system: Cannot exchange either matter or energy with surroundings

State Variables

  • Extensive properties: Depend on the amount of substance (mass, volume, energy)
  • Intensive properties: Independent of the amount of substance (temperature, pressure, density)
  • State functions: Properties that depend only on the current state, not on the path taken to reach that state (internal energy, enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs free energy)

Example: When you heat 100 mL of water from 25°C to 50°C, the change in temperature (ΔT = 25°C) is the same regardless of whether you heat it quickly or slowly. Temperature is a state function. However, the heat required depends on the path (e.g., heating at constant pressure vs. constant volume).

First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy Conservation

The first law of thermodynamics is essentially the law of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems.

ΔU = q + w
  • ΔU: Change in internal energy of the system
  • q: Heat transferred to the system (positive when absorbed)
  • w: Work done on the system (positive when done on the system)

Isothermal Expansion of an Ideal Gas

w = -nRT ln(V₂/V₁) = -nRT ln(P₁/P₂)
  • For expansion: V₂ > V₁, so w is negative (work done by the system)
  • For compression: V₂ < V₁, so w is positive (work done on the system)

Mnemonic: "CUTE"

  • Can't create or destroy energy
  • Universal conservation principle
  • Transfer between forms is possible
  • Energy total remains constant

Heat of Reaction and Enthalpy

Enthalpy (H)

H = U + PV
ΔH = ΔU + Δ(PV)
At constant pressure: ΔH = qₚ
  • Exothermic reaction: ΔH < 0 (heat released)
  • Endothermic reaction: ΔH > 0 (heat absorbed)

Hess's Law

The enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the pathway between the initial and final states.

Example calculation:

  • For the reaction: C + O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -393.5 kJ/mol
  • For the reaction: C + ½O₂ → CO, ΔH = -110.5 kJ/mol
  • For the reaction: CO + ½O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -283.0 kJ/mol
  • Verification: -110.5 kJ/mol + (-283.0 kJ/mol) = -393.5 kJ/mol

Spontaneity and Entropy

Entropy (S)

Entropy is a measure of molecular disorder or randomness in a system.

S = k ln W

where k is Boltzmann's constant and W is the number of possible microstates.

Entropy changes:

  • Solid → Liquid → Gas (increasing entropy)
  • Dissolution (usually increases entropy)
  • Mixing (increases entropy)
  • Reactions that increase the number of moles of gas (increase entropy)

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The total entropy of an isolated system always increases for spontaneous processes.

ΔS_total = ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings ≥ 0
For the surroundings: ΔS_surroundings = -ΔH_system/T
Combined expression: ΔS_total = ΔS_system - ΔH_system/T

Free Energy and Equilibrium

Gibbs Free Energy (G)

G = H - TS
ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

Criteria for spontaneity:

  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous process
  • ΔG = 0: System at equilibrium
  • ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous process (reverse process is spontaneous)

Relationship Between ΔG and Equilibrium

ΔG° = -RT ln K
ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q

where K is the equilibrium constant and Q is the reaction quotient.

At equilibrium: ΔG = 0 and Q = K

Mnemonic: "GHOST"

  • G = H - TS
  • Gibbs free energy determines spontaneity
  • Heat content (enthalpy) measures energy transfer
  • Order decreases as entropy increases
  • Spontaneous when ΔG is negative
  • Temperature affects the balance between ΔH and ΔS

Predicting Spontaneity

Memory Aid: "The PETS Rule"

  • Positive ΔH, Positive ΔS: Spontaneous at high T
  • Positive ΔH, Negative ΔS: Never spontaneous
  • Negative ΔH, Positive ΔS: Always spontaneous
  • Negative ΔH, Negative ΔS: Spontaneous at low T

Example 1: Spontaneity of Ice Melting

Consider ice melting at room temperature (25°C):

  • ΔH = +6.01 kJ/mol (endothermic)
  • ΔS = +22.1 J/(mol·K) (disorder increases)
  • ΔG = ΔH - TΔS = +6.01 kJ/mol - (298.15 K × 0.0221 kJ/(mol·K)) = -0.58 kJ/mol
  • Since ΔG < 0, the process is spontaneous at room temperature
  • At temperatures below 0°C, ΔG becomes positive, and the process is non-spontaneous

Example 2: Combustion of Methane

For the reaction: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

  • ΔH° = -890.3 kJ/mol (highly exothermic)
  • ΔS° = -242.1 J/(mol·K) (decrease in disorder due to gas → liquid)
  • At 25°C: ΔG° = -890.3 kJ/mol - (298.15 K × -0.2421 kJ/(mol·K)) = -818.1 kJ/mol
  • The large negative ΔG° explains why methane combustion is highly favorable
