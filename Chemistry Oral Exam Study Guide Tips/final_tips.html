Final Preparation Tips

Oral Exam Strategies

Before the Exam

  • Prepare a mental roadmap for each major topic, organizing key points in a logical sequence
  • Practice explaining concepts to a friend or even to yourself in a mirror
  • Record yourself explaining difficult topics and listen to identify areas for improvement
  • Anticipate questions the professor might ask based on the emphasis in class
  • Prepare concise definitions for all key terms

During the Exam

  • Listen carefully to the question before answering
  • Take a moment to organize your thoughts before speaking
  • Start with the definition when asked about a concept
  • Use examples to illustrate your understanding
  • Draw clear diagrams when explaining structures or mechanisms
  • Connect topics to show comprehensive understanding
  • If unsure, acknowledge what you know and what you're uncertain about

Remember:

The professor is not trying to trick you but to assess your understanding. Demonstrate your knowledge clearly and confidently, and don't be afraid to think through problems step by step.

Frequently Asked Topics

Based on the professor's materials, these topics are likely to be emphasized in your oral exam:

Inorganic Chemistry

  • Thermodynamics: Gibbs free energy calculations, spontaneity of reactions
  • Kinetics: Rate laws, reaction mechanisms, Arrhenius equation
  • Electrochemistry: Cell potentials, Nernst equation, electrochemical cells

Organic Chemistry

  • Stereochemistry: R/S configuration, optical activity
  • Reaction mechanisms: SN1/SN2, electrophilic aromatic substitution
  • Functional group transformations: Oxidation/reduction, nucleophilic addition

Biochemistry

  • Carbohydrates: Monosaccharide structures, glycosidic bonds
  • NAD and FAD: Structure and role in biological redox reactions

Drawing Practice

The professor specifically mentioned that you should be able to draw structures and diagrams. Practice drawing these key structures:

Must-Practice Drawings

  • Electrochemical cells: Galvanic and electrolytic cells with proper labeling
  • Reaction energy diagrams: Showing activation energy and transition states
  • Organic reaction mechanisms: Using curved arrows to show electron movement
  • Carbohydrate structures: Haworth projections of glucose, galactose, etc.
  • NAD+ and NADH: Complete structures showing the redox-active part
  • Molecular orbital diagrams: For simple molecules

Tip:

Practice drawing these structures multiple times until you can reproduce them quickly and accurately from memory. This will give you confidence during the exam.

Memory Techniques for Last-Minute Review

Spaced Repetition

Review material at increasing intervals: first after 1 hour, then after 1 day, then after 3 days. This strengthens memory more effectively than cramming.

Active Recall

Test yourself rather than simply re-reading. Close your notes and try to explain concepts in your own words.

Memory Palace

Associate key concepts with locations in a familiar place (like your home). As you mentally walk through the space, recall the associated information.

Chunking

Group related information together. For example, remember the steps of glycolysis as three main phases rather than ten individual steps.

Teach Someone Else

Explaining concepts to others (even imaginary students) forces you to organize your knowledge and identify gaps in understanding.

Day Before the Exam

Do:

  • Review your summary notes and flashcards
  • Practice drawing key structures one last time
  • Get a good night's sleep (7-8 hours)
  • Prepare everything you need for the exam day
  • Eat a balanced meal and stay hydrated
  • Do light exercise to reduce stress
  • Visualize yourself successfully answering questions

Don't:

  • Start learning new material
  • Stay up late cramming
  • Consume excessive caffeine or alcohol
  • Compare your preparation with others
  • Focus on what you don't know

Remember:

Your brain needs rest to consolidate memories. A well-rested mind will perform better than an exhausted one filled with last-minute information.
