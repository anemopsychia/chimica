Organic Chemistry: Core Concepts

Organic chemistry is a critical component of your oral exam. This section covers the essential concepts of organic chemistry with a focus on the topics emphasized in your professor's materials.

Alkanes

Structure and Properties

  • Definition: Saturated hydrocarbons with single bonds only
  • Types:
    • Normal (straight-chain)
    • Branched
    • Cyclic
  • Hybridization: sp³ for all carbon atoms
  • Geometry: Tetrahedral with bond angles of 109.5°
  • Conformations: Different spatial arrangements due to rotation around single bonds
    • Staggered: Lower energy, more stable
    • Eclipsed: Higher energy, less stable

Nomenclature

  • IUPAC rules:
    1. Identify the longest carbon chain
    2. Number the chain to give substituents the lowest possible numbers
    3. Name substituents as prefixes (e.g., methyl-, ethyl-)
    4. List substituents alphabetically

Reactions

  • Combustion: Complete oxidation to CO₂ and H₂O
  • Halogenation: Radical substitution with halogens (X₂)
    • Initiation: X₂ → 2X• (by heat or light)
    • Propagation: X• + RH → HX + R•, R• + X₂ → RX + X•
    • Termination: X• + X• → X₂, R• + X• → RX, R• + R• → R-R

Isomerism

Constitutional Isomerism

  • Definition: Compounds with the same molecular formula but different connectivity
  • Types:
    • Chain isomers: Different carbon skeleton
    • Position isomers: Different position of functional groups
    • Functional group isomers: Different functional groups

Stereoisomerism

  • Definition: Compounds with the same connectivity but different spatial arrangement
  • Types:
    • Conformational isomers: Interconvertible by rotation around single bonds
    • Configurational isomers: Not interconvertible without breaking bonds

Optical Isomerism

  • Chiral centers: Carbon atoms bonded to four different groups
  • Enantiomers: Mirror images that are not superimposable
  • Optical activity: Ability to rotate plane-polarized light
  • R/S system: Method for designating configuration
    1. Assign priorities to substituents (higher atomic number = higher priority)
    2. Orient molecule with lowest priority group away from observer
    3. Trace path from highest to lowest priority (excluding the group pointing away)
    4. Clockwise path = R configuration, counterclockwise = S configuration

Tip: Visualizing Stereochemistry

Use your hands to model chiral centers:

  • Four fingers represent the four different groups
  • Thumb points in the direction of the lowest priority group
  • Trace a path from highest to lowest priority with your other hand
  • If you trace clockwise, it's R; counterclockwise, it's S

Diastereomers

  • Definition: Stereoisomers that are not mirror images
  • Meso compounds: Molecules with chiral centers but with an internal plane of symmetry
  • E/Z system: For designating configuration of double bonds
    • E (entgegen): Higher priority groups on opposite sides
    • Z (zusammen): Higher priority groups on same side

Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

SN2 Mechanism

  • Characteristics:
    • Single-step mechanism
    • Bimolecular (rate depends on both nucleophile and substrate)
    • Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]
    • Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
    • Transition state has pentavalent carbon
  • Factors affecting SN2:
    • Steric hindrance: Primary > secondary > tertiary substrates
    • Nucleophile strength: Stronger nucleophiles favor SN2
    • Solvent: Aprotic solvents favor SN2

SN1 Mechanism

  • Characteristics:
    • Two-step mechanism
    • Monomolecular (rate depends only on substrate)
    • Rate = k[substrate]
    • Carbocation intermediate
    • Racemization (or partial inversion)
  • Factors affecting SN1:
    • Carbocation stability: Tertiary > secondary > primary
    • Solvent: Protic solvents favor SN1
    • Leaving group ability: Better leaving groups favor SN1

Mnemonic: "S-ONE-S-TWO"

  • S₁: One-step, No stereochemistry, Excellent with tertiary substrates
  • S₂: Two molecules in rate-determining step, Walden inversion, Optimal with primary substrates

Example: SN1 vs. SN2 in Drug Metabolism

Consider the metabolism of alkylating agents:

  • Primary alkyl halides undergo SN2 reactions with nucleophiles in the body
  • Tertiary alkyl halides undergo SN1 reactions, forming carbocations
  • These carbocations can react with DNA, potentially causing mutations
  • This explains why some alkylating agents are carcinogenic

Alkenes and Alkynes

Structure and Properties

Alkenes

  • Carbon-carbon double bond
  • sp² hybridization at double-bonded carbons
  • Planar geometry with 120° bond angles
  • Restricted rotation around double bond

Alkynes

  • Carbon-carbon triple bond
  • sp hybridization at triple-bonded carbons
  • Linear geometry with 180° bond angles

Stability of Alkenes

  • Factors affecting stability:
    • Degree of substitution: Tetrasubstituted > trisubstituted > disubstituted > monosubstituted
    • Hyperconjugation: Stabilization by adjacent C-H bonds
    • Electron-donating groups increase stability
    • Trans isomers generally more stable than cis isomers
