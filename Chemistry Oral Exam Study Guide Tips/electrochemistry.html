Inorganic Chemistry: Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry is the study of the relationship between electricity and chemical reactions. It's one of the most frequently tested topics in your oral exam and requires understanding both theoretical concepts and practical applications.

Important: This is one of the most frequently tested topics in your oral exam! Make sure you understand the principles of electrochemical cells, can calculate cell potentials, and can draw cell diagrams correctly.

Electrolytic Conduction

Electrolytes

  • Definition: Substances that dissociate into ions in solution or molten state
  • Strong electrolytes: Completely dissociate (e.g., NaCl, HCl, NaOH)
  • Weak electrolytes: Partially dissociate (e.g., CH₃COOH, NH₃)
  • Non-electrolytes: Do not dissociate (e.g., glucose, alcohol)

Conductivity

  • Ionic conductivity: Movement of ions in solution
  • Factors affecting conductivity:
    • Concentration of ions
    • Mobility of ions
    • Temperature
    • Nature of solvent

Electrolysis

Principles

  • Definition: Process where electrical energy drives a non-spontaneous chemical reaction
  • Components:
    • Electrolytic cell
    • External power source
    • Electrodes (anode and cathode)
    • Electrolyte

Electrolysis of Molten Salts

Example: Electrolysis of molten NaCl

  • Cathode (reduction): Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na
  • Anode (oxidation): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
  • Overall: 2NaCl → 2Na + Cl₂

Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions

  • Competing reactions: Water can be reduced or oxidized
  • Cathode reactions:
    • Metal ion reduction: M^n+ + ne⁻ → M
    • Water reduction: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻
  • Anode reactions:
    • Anion oxidation: 2X⁻ → X₂ + 2e⁻
    • Water oxidation: 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻
  • Preferential discharge: Depends on standard reduction potentials and concentrations

Quantitative Aspects of Electrolysis

Faraday's Laws

  • First law: The mass of a substance produced at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed
  • Second law: For the same quantity of electricity, the masses of different substances produced are proportional to their equivalent weights
  • Faraday constant (F): 96,485 C/mol (charge carried by one mole of electrons)

Calculations

m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)
  • m = mass deposited (g)
  • M = molar mass (g/mol)
  • I = current (A)
  • t = time (s)
  • n = number of electrons transferred
  • F = Faraday constant (96,485 C/mol)

Electrochemical Cells

Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

  • Definition: Cells that convert chemical energy to electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions
  • Components:
    • Anode (oxidation occurs)
    • Cathode (reduction occurs)
    • Salt bridge or porous barrier
    • External circuit

Mnemonic: "RED CAT, AN OX"

  • REDuction at the CAThode
  • ANode is where OXidation occurs

Cell Notation

  • Convention: Anode | anode solution || cathode solution | cathode
  • Example: Zn | Zn²⁺ || Cu²⁺ | Cu

Cell Potential

  • Definition: Measure of the driving force for the cell reaction
  • Standard cell potential: E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode
  • Relationship to Gibbs free energy: ΔG° = -nFE°cell
  • Spontaneity: E°cell > 0 indicates a spontaneous reaction

Mnemonic: "SPECS"

  • Salt bridge connects half-cells
  • Positive electrode is the cathode
  • Electrons flow from anode to cathode
  • Cations move toward cathode
  • Spontaneous reactions have positive cell potentials

Electrode Potential

Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)

  • Components:
    • Platinum electrode
    • 1M H₃O⁺ solution
    • H₂ gas at 1 atm pressure
  • Convention: Assigned a potential of 0.00 V
  • Half-reaction: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ ⇌ H₂

Standard Reduction Potentials

  • Definition: Potential of a half-cell compared to the SHE under standard conditions
  • Standard conditions:
    • 25°C (298.15 K)
    • 1M concentration for solutions
    • 1 atm pressure for gases
    • Pure solids and liquids
  • Table of values: Higher values indicate greater tendency to be reduced

Relationship Between Reduction and Oxidation Potentials

  • Oxidation potential: Negative of the reduction potential
  • Example: If E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = -0.76 V, then E°(Zn/Zn²⁺) = +0.76 V

Tip: Understanding Standard Reduction Potentials

  • Think of reduction potentials as a measure of how badly an element "wants" electrons
  • Elements with more positive potentials (like F₂, +2.87 V) strongly attract electrons
  • Elements with more negative potentials (like Li, -3.05 V) easily give up electrons
  • In any reaction, the species with higher reduction potential will be reduced

Redox Potential

Determining Reaction Direction

  • Rule: Species with higher reduction potential will be reduced; species with lower reduction potential will be oxidized
  • Example: Cu²⁺ + Zn → Cu + Zn²⁺
    • E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V
    • E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = -0.76 V
    • Cu²⁺ has higher reduction potential, so it will be reduced
