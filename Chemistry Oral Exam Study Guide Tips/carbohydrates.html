Biochemistry Propedeutics: Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are one of the most frequently tested topics in your oral exam. This section covers their structure, classification, and properties with a focus on the concepts emphasized by your professor.

Important: Carbohydrates are frequently tested in your oral exam! Pay special attention to monosaccharide structures, stereochemistry, and the differences between common disaccharides.

Monosaccharides

Classification

By carbon number

  • Trioses (3C): Glyceraldehyde, dihydroxyacetone
  • Tetroses (4C): Erythrose, threose
  • Pentoses (5C): Ribose, xylose, arabinose
  • Hexoses (6C): Glucose, galactose, mannose, fructose

By functional group

  • Aldoses: Contain aldehyde group (e.g., glucose)
  • Ketoses: Contain ketone group (e.g., fructose)

Mnemonic: "GALP"

  • Glucose: 6-carbon aldose, most common
  • Aldoses have aldehyde group
  • Length determines classification (tri-, tetr-, pent-, hex-)
  • Pentoses and hexoses form rings

Stereochemistry

  • Chiral centers: Most carbons in monosaccharides are chiral
  • D/L system: Based on configuration of the highest-numbered chiral carbon
    • D: Hydroxyl group on right in Fischer projection
    • L: Hydroxyl group on left in Fischer projection
  • Most naturally occurring sugars: D-configuration

Epimers

  • Definition: Stereoisomers that differ at only one chiral center
  • Examples:
    • Glucose and mannose (differ at C-2)
    • Glucose and galactose (differ at C-4)

Mnemonic: "GAME"

  • Glucose and Mannose are epimers at C-2
  • Glucose and Allose are epimers at C-3
  • Glucose and Galactose are epimers at C-4
  • Epimers differ at only one stereocenter

Cyclization and Ring Structures

Cyclization

  • Mechanism: Intramolecular reaction between carbonyl and hydroxyl groups
  • Ring sizes:
    • Pyranose: Six-membered ring (like pyran)
    • Furanose: Five-membered ring (like furan)
  • Anomeric carbon: The former carbonyl carbon
  • Anomers:
    • α-anomer: Hydroxyl group at anomeric carbon is trans to CH₂OH
    • β-anomer: Hydroxyl group at anomeric carbon is cis to CH₂OH

Tip: Understanding Anomeric Carbon

  • The anomeric carbon is the former carbonyl carbon (C-1 in aldoses, C-2 in ketoses)
  • It's the only carbon that can exist in two different configurations (α and β)
  • In Haworth projections:
    • α: hydroxyl group points down (opposite the CH₂OH)
    • β: hydroxyl group points up (same side as CH₂OH)
  • β-forms are generally more stable due to equatorial positioning of groups

Haworth Projections

  • Representation: Three-dimensional ring structure in two dimensions
  • Conventions:
    • Ring is viewed from the side
    • Heavier line represents the side closer to viewer
    • Groups above the plane point up, groups below point down
    • In D-sugars, the CH₂OH group points up

Memory Aid: "RIGHT DOWN"

  • RIGHT: Anomeric carbon is on the right
  • DOWN: In α-anomers, OH points down from the anomeric carbon
  • In β-anomers, OH points up from the anomeric carbon

Tip: Visualizing Carbohydrate Structures

Use your hand to model pyranose rings:

  • Make a fist with thumb extended
  • The thumb represents the CH₂OH group
  • In D-sugars, the thumb points up in Haworth projections
  • In L-sugars, the thumb points down

Mutarotation

  • Definition: Spontaneous change in optical rotation due to interconversion between anomers
  • Mechanism:
    • Ring opening to form open-chain structure
    • Ring closure to form either α or β anomer
  • Equilibrium mixture: Contains both anomers and small amount of open-chain form
  • Example: α-D-glucose ([α]D = +112.2°) → equilibrium mixture ([α]D = +52.7°)

Example: Glucose Mutarotation

When crystalline α-D-glucose (specific rotation [α]D = +112.2°) is dissolved in water:

  • The optical rotation gradually changes to +52.7°
  • This represents an equilibrium mixture of 36% α-form and 64% β-form
  • The process involves ring opening to the open-chain form followed by ring closure
  • This explains why freshly prepared glucose solutions show changing optical rotation

Derivatives of Monosaccharides

Sugar Alcohols

  • Formation: Reduction of aldehyde or ketone group
  • Examples:
    • Sorbitol (from glucose)
    • Mannitol (from mannose)
    • Xylitol (from xylose)
  • Properties: Sweet taste, not metabolized by many bacteria (used in sugar-free products)

Sugar Acids

  • Oxidation of aldehyde group: Aldonic acids (e.g., gluconic acid)
  • Oxidation of primary alcohol: Uronic acids (e.g., glucuronic acid)
  • Oxidation of both ends: Aldaric acids (e.g., glucaric acid)

Amino Sugars

  • Structure: Hydroxyl group replaced by amino group
  • Examples:
    • Glucosamine (component of chitin)
    • Galactosamine (component of chondroitin sulfate)
  • Derivatives: N-acetylated amino sugars (e.g., N-acetylglucosamine)

Deoxy Sugars

  • Structure: Hydroxyl group replaced by hydrogen
  • Example: 2-deoxyribose (component of DNA)

Disaccharides

Formation

  • Glycosidic bond: Formed between anomeric carbon of one sugar and hydroxyl group of another
  • Classification:
    • α(1→4): e.g., maltose
    • β(1→4): e.g., cellobiose
    • α(1→2): e.g., sucrose
